Urban Stormwater

Urban stormwater is where many communities first experience the impacts of extreme rainfall and aging infrastructure. The GGBS Framework helps integrate Green, Grey, Blue, and Smart measures to reduce flooding, control pollutants, and improve urban livability.

Challenges in Urban Stormwater

As cities densify and impervious cover increases, more rainfall is converted into rapid runoff. Combined with aging drainage networks and more intense storms, this leads to:

  • Frequent street and basement flooding;
  • Overloaded storm and combined sewer systems;
  • Water-quality degradation in receiving waters;
  • Local heat and habitat loss from reduced vegetation and open water.

Traditional responses focus on enlarging pipes and building more storage. The GGBS Framework broadens the toolbox, emphasizing how green, grey, blue, and smart measures can be combined at multiple scales.

GGBS Perspective on Urban Catchments

In an urban stormwater context, each GGBS dimension plays a specific role:

  • Green reduces runoff generation and improves local conditions;
  • Grey provides backbone conveyance and storage capacity;
  • Blue includes creeks, rivers, ponds, and lakes that receive runoff;
  • Smart uses data and models to plan, monitor, and operate systems.

The aim is not to replace grey with green, or vice versa, but to design a portfolio of measures that work together for reliability, cost efficiency, and co-benefits.

A key principle is to manage stormwater as a resource where possible, while reducing flood damages and pollution where risks are highest.

Examples of Integrated Urban Stormwater Strategies

Green streets and alleys Retrofitting curbs, planters, and permeable parking lanes to capture runoff, reduce peak flows, and improve streetscape quality.
Green–grey drainage corridors Using bioswales and vegetated channels alongside conventional storm sewers to slow, infiltrate, and convey runoff.
Distributed roofs and courtyards Green roofs, rain barrels, and courtyard rain gardens that intercept rainfall across many small sites.
Detention with blue–green amenity Stormwater basins, ponds, or linear water features designed as public spaces rather than hidden infrastructure.
Smart-controlled storage Using sensors and controls to dynamically adjust outflows from tanks or basins in response to forecasted rainfall and observed flows.
Targeted CSO reductions Combining green retrofits and storage near combined sewer areas to reduce overflow frequency and volume.

Scales of Implementation

Urban stormwater applications of GGBS can be organized by scale:

  • Site scale: individual buildings, parcels, and campus areas implementing measures such as rain gardens, green roofs, and permeable pavements.
  • Neighborhood or district scale: coordinated green streets, pocket parks, and shared detention facilities.
  • City or watershed scale: integrated planning of drainage networks, storage, and receiving water capacity, supported by models and monitoring.

Planning at multiple scales helps ensure that local actions contribute to broader flood and water-quality objectives.

Role of Data, Models, and Monitoring

Smart tools play an important role in urban stormwater management:

  • Continuous rainfall and flow monitoring to detect hotspots;
  • Hydrologic and hydraulic models to test green–grey portfolios;
  • Scenario analysis under different climate and land-use conditions;
  • Real-time control strategies to use storage more efficiently;
  • Performance evaluation of implemented measures over time.

These tools help prioritize investments, refine designs, and support adaptive management as conditions evolve.

Linkages to Other GGBS Applications

Urban stormwater does not exist in isolation. Runoff generated in cities affects downstream rivers, estuaries, and coastal waters. Within GGBS:

  • Urban stormwater strategies connect directly to Watershed Management through flow and load contributions.
  • Discharges to tidal rivers and bays connect to Estuaries & Tidal Rivers and Coasts & Shorelines.
  • Many urban projects can be framed as part of broader Flood Risk & Resilience strategies.
