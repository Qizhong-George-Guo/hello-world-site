Flood Risk & Resilience

Floods arise from intense rainfall, swollen rivers, coastal storms, and their combinations. The GGBS Framework supports integrated strategies for pluvial, fluvial, and coastal flooding, with a focus on risk reduction, recovery, and long-term resilience.

From Hazard to Risk to Resilience

Flood risk is more than water levels or discharge. It reflects the combination of:

  • Hazard – the probability and intensity of flooding;
  • Exposure – people, assets, and systems in harm’s way;
  • Vulnerability – susceptibility to damage and loss;
  • Capacity – ability to prepare, respond, and recover.

Resilience emphasizes not only reducing damages, but also speeding recovery, maintaining essential functions, and adapting over time.

GGBS Lens on Flood Risk

The GGBS Framework views flood risk through four interacting dimensions:

  • Green – measures that retain, infiltrate, and slow water;
  • Grey – levees, walls, tunnels, pumps, and drainage networks;
  • Blue – rivers, floodplains, wetlands, estuaries, and coasts;
  • Smart – monitoring, models, early warning, and decision support.
A resilient strategy balances investments across these dimensions rather than relying on a single “silver bullet” solution.

Types of Flooding in the GGBS Framework

Pluvial flooding Short-duration, intense rainfall that overwhelms local drainage, causing street and basement flooding even away from rivers and coasts.
Fluvial flooding River and stream overflows when upstream runoff and reservoir operations exceed channel and floodplain capacity.
Coastal & surge flooding High water levels and waves driven by storms, tides, and sea-level rise along open coasts and estuary mouths.
Compound flooding Combinations of rainfall, river flows, surge, and tides that occur together, especially in estuaries and low-lying coastal cities.

GGBS Strategies for Flood Risk Reduction

Representative strategies across the GGBS dimensions include:

  • Green: distributed GI, reforestation, soil rehabilitation, and wetland restoration to reduce runoff and store water.
  • Grey: levees, seawalls, floodwalls, tunnels, detention, and diversion channels to provide robust protection and conveyance.
  • Blue: reconnecting floodplains, creating bypass channels, and using flood-compatible land uses in high-risk zones.
  • Smart: early warning systems, real-time control of gates and pumps, and scenario-based planning tools.

In practice, portfolios mix these measures to meet local objectives and constraints.

Planning, Equity, and Governance

Effective flood-risk management also requires:

  • Clear objectives that consider safety, environment, and cost;
  • Attention to who benefits and who remains at risk;
  • Land-use planning and zoning aligned with flood hazards;
  • Coordination across agencies and jurisdictions;
  • Engagement with communities throughout the planning process.

Resilience planning recognizes that not all risk can be eliminated; it focuses on reducing unacceptable risk and improving recovery capacity.

Role of Data, Models, and Smart Systems

Smart tools support each phase of the flood-risk cycle:

  • Risk assessment: hydrologic, hydraulic, and surge modeling; inundation mapping; damage and loss estimation.
  • Monitoring and forecasting: rainfall, river levels, groundwater, tide and surge monitoring; short-term and seasonal forecasts.
  • Early warning and response: thresholds, alerts, evacuation planning, and real-time operations of gates, pumps, and reservoirs.
  • Adaptive management: post-event analysis, updating models, and revising strategies as conditions change.

These capabilities link directly to the Data, Models & AI application area.

Connections to Other GGBS Applications

Flood risk and resilience are cross-cutting themes that draw on multiple application areas:

  • Urban Stormwater addresses pluvial flooding in cities.
  • Watersheds address fluvial flooding and upstream–downstream interactions.
  • Estuaries & Tidal Rivers and Coasts & Shorelines address surge, tides, and compound flooding.
  • Sponge City / Low-Impact provides an example of integrated flood-resilience strategies in urbanizing regions.
