Coasts & Shorelines

Coasts are shaped by waves, tides, currents, sediment transport, storms, and sea-level rise. The GGBS Framework integrates beaches, dunes, wetlands, engineered structures, and smart tools to create resilient, adaptive coastal systems.

Why Coasts Matter

Coastal zones are home to major cities, ports, industries, tourism, and critical ecosystems. They face multiple hazards:

  • Storm surge and coastal inundation;
  • Wave-driven erosion and overwash;
  • Longshore sediment imbalances;
  • Sea-level rise and subsidence;
  • Loss of beaches, dunes, and wetlands.

Managing these challenges requires integrating engineering, natural systems, and forecasting tools across spatial and temporal scales.

GGBS View of Coastal Systems

At the coast:

  • Green includes dunes, wetlands, mangroves, and reefs that attenuate waves and store sediment;
  • Grey includes seawalls, revetments, groins, jetties, and breakwaters;
  • Blue includes the nearshore, surf zone, estuary mouths, and offshore wave fields;
  • Smart includes wave buoys, tide gauges, surge models, and real-time forecasting.
Coastal resilience is strongest when natural and engineered systems are co-designed to work together, guided by robust data and models.

Key Coastal Processes and Questions

Wave transformation How do waves shoal, refract, break, and run up beaches? How do changes in offshore wave climate affect shoreline stability?
Longshore sediment transport How do waves and currents move sediment along the coast, and how do structures alter these pathways?
Beach and dune dynamics How do dunes, berms, and profiles evolve through storms, recovery, and nourishment cycles?
Storm surge and coastal flooding How do tides, surge, waves, river flows, and sea-level rise combine to produce compound flooding?
Wetland and barrier-island evolution How do wetlands, tidal flats, and barrier systems migrate or collapse under changing sediment supply and sea-level rise?

GGBS Coastal Strategies

Examples of integrated coastal strategies include:

  • Beach nourishment to restore protective profiles and buffer waves and surge;
  • Dune restoration and fencing to increase elevation, enhance vegetation, and trap sand;
  • Living shorelines using marshes, reefs, and hybrid edges to reduce erosion and enhance habitat;
  • Breakwaters and detached structures to reduce wave energy while maintaining natural sand transport where possible;
  • Groins and jetties used carefully to manage longshore sediment transport at inlets and harbor entrances;
  • Hybrid green–grey protection combining dunes, wetlands, and engineered defenses for multiple hazards.

Role of Smart Tools

Coastal applications rely heavily on monitoring and modeling:

  • Wave buoys, tide gauges, and offshore sensors;
  • High-resolution bathymetry, topography, and shoreline change data;
  • Spectral wave models and storm-surge models;
  • Beach-profile monitoring and sediment budgets;
  • Scenario-based planning for sea-level rise and storms;
  • Decision-support tools for selecting hybrid and nature-based options.

Smart systems ensure that coastal decisions are grounded in data and adapt to changing climate and geomorphic trends.
