Overview of the GGBS Framework

The GGBS Framework organizes green, grey, blue, and smart infrastructure into a single, integrated way of thinking about water and urban systems. This page introduces why the framework is needed, how it is structured, and how it can be used in practice.

Related pages: Green Grey Blue Smart / Intelligent

Why an Integrated Framework?

Water and urban systems are shaped by multiple interacting forces: climate variability and change, land-use development, aging infrastructure, ecological degradation, and evolving social and economic conditions. Green infrastructure, grey infrastructure, blue-water systems, and digital technologies are often planned and operated in isolation, even though they share the same watersheds, neighborhoods, and coastlines.

The GGBS Framework responds to this challenge by providing a system-scale lens. Instead of optimizing one type of infrastructure at a time, GGBS encourages:

  • Co-design of nature-based and engineered measures;
  • Recognition of rivers, estuaries, and coasts as active system components;
  • Use of data, models, and intelligent tools as part of the infrastructure system;
  • Planning that considers multiple hazards, time scales, and scenarios.

Relationship to Existing Concepts

GGBS is designed to complement—not replace—existing ideas such as green infrastructure, Sponge Cities, nature-based solutions, and integrated water resources management (IWRM). It offers a simple four-part language for bringing these ideas together in a consistent way.

In particular, GGBS:

  • Highlights how green and grey solutions can be combined rather than viewed as alternatives;
  • Places rivers, lakes, wetlands, estuaries, and coasts (blue) at the center of system thinking;
  • Elevates smart and intelligent components to the same level as physical works.
In short: GGBS provides a compact framework that is easy to explain, flexible enough for different contexts, and grounded in hydrologic, hydraulic, ecological, and systems-engineering principles.

The Four Dimensions at a Glance

At the core of the framework are four complementary dimensions. Each has its own role, but the value of GGBS lies in how they are combined for planning, design, and operation.

  • Green: Nature-based solutions that infiltrate, store, slow, and treat water while providing co-benefits such as cooling, habitat, and improved public space.
  • Grey: Engineered and structural systems—sewers, culverts, tunnels, levees, dams, reservoirs, and coastal structures— that provide capacity, robustness, and controlled conveyance.
  • Blue: Rivers, lakes, wetlands, estuaries, and coastal waters that convey flow and sediment, store floodwaters, and support ecosystems, navigation, and recreation.
  • Smart: Monitoring networks, models, data platforms, decision-support tools, and AI-based analytics that allow systems to be observed, understood, and managed adaptively. This dimension incorporates the intelligent systems concepts previously developed under GGBI.

Individual projects may focus on one dimension more than others, but system-scale resilience typically emerges when all four are considered together.

System-Scale and Multi-Hazard Thinking

The GGBS Framework is particularly useful when multiple hazards or processes interact—for example:

  • Pluvial flooding in dense urban neighborhoods;
  • Fluvial flooding from rivers and reservoirs;
  • Coastal storm surge and sea-level rise;
  • Compound flooding where these processes overlap;
  • Linked sediment, water-quality, and ecological impacts.

By using the same four-part structure across urban catchments, watersheds, and coastal zones, GGBS helps students, practitioners, and decision-makers compare options, identify synergies, and communicate complex ideas more clearly.

How to Use This Framework

GGBS can be applied at different levels of detail, from conceptual planning to detailed analysis:

  • For students: A mental model for organizing topics in hydrology, hydraulics, stormwater management, river/coastal processes, and data/AI applications.
  • For practitioners: A checklist and structure for exploring alternative strategies, combining measures, and communicating with stakeholders.
  • For researchers: A way to situate case studies and methods within a broader system, and to identify gaps and opportunities at the interfaces of green, grey, blue, and smart systems.
