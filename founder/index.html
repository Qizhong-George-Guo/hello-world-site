Background & Professional Focus

Qizhong (George) Guo is a professor of civil and environmental engineering at Rutgers, The State University of New Jersey. His work spans urban stormwater management, watershed hydrology, estuarine and coastal processes, and flood resilience.

Over the course of his career, he has been involved in:

  • Design, analysis, and monitoring of green stormwater infrastructure;
  • Watershed and basin-scale management strategies;
  • Estuarine and coastal hydrodynamics, waves, and sediment transport;
  • Assessment of flood risk across pluvial, fluvial, and coastal domains;
  • Teaching and mentoring at the graduate and undergraduate levels.

Much of this work has been carried out in collaboration with cities, utilities, agencies, and international partners.

Why GGBS?

The GGBS Framework grew out of decades of research, teaching, and project work in which “green infrastructure,” “grey infrastructure,” “blue systems,” and “smart tools” were often discussed separately but needed to function together in the real world.

GGBS was created to:

  • Provide a simple, memorable way to think about integrated infrastructure;
  • Connect site-scale practices to watershed, estuary, and coastal systems;
  • Bridge engineering design, environmental performance, and social goals;
  • Give students, practitioners, and decision-makers a common language.

The framework is intentionally practical: it is designed to support real projects, policies, and teaching modules, not only conceptual diagrams.

GGBS is not a replacement for existing methods or standards. It is a way to organize them, see gaps, and design portfolios that combine Green, Grey, Blue, and Smart elements more deliberately.

Threads Across Research, Teaching & Practice

The GGBS Framework reflects several recurring threads in Professor Guo’s work:

  • Urban stormwater and green infrastructure – from lab experiments and field monitoring to planning and design guidance for cities.
  • Watershed and basin management – including large-scale strategies for river basins and their tributaries.
  • Estuaries and coasts – tides, storm surge, waves, sediment, and shoreline change.
  • Flood risk and resilience – integrating pluvial, fluvial, and coastal flooding in risk assessment and mitigation.
  • Education and mentoring – courses on green infrastructure, hydrology, and coastal engineering, plus supervision of numerous student projects and theses.

Collaboration & Knowledge Sharing

GGBS.ai is intended as a collaborative space, not just a personal portfolio. Over time, the site will:

  • Collect and organize publications, reports, and teaching materials;
  • Share case studies from different regions and contexts;
  • Provide tools, datasets, and example workflows;
  • Highlight student work and partner contributions where appropriate.

The aim is to make practical knowledge easier to find and reuse, and to encourage dialogue across disciplines and sectors.

Working With GGBS

Potential ways to engage with the GGBS Framework include:

  • Using the framework to structure local or regional resilience plans;
  • Incorporating GGBS diagrams and concepts into teaching materials;
  • Collaborating on case studies or comparative analyses;
  • Exploring integration with digital tools, models, and AI workflows.
