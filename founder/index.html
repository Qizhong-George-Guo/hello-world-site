Background & Professional Focus

Qizhong (George) Guo is a professor of civil and environmental engineering at Rutgers University–New Brunswick, New Jersey, U.S.A. His work centers on the intertwined challenges of water, environment, and cities.

His core areas of expertise include:

  • Urban stormwater and green infrastructure;
  • Watershed hydrology and sediment processes;
  • Tidal rivers, estuaries, waves, and coastal morphodynamics;
  • Flood risk and resilience across pluvial, fluvial, and coastal hazards;
  • Graduate and undergraduate teaching, mentoring, and curriculum development.

His work bridges scientific research, engineering design, field monitoring, and policy-relevant analysis, with collaborations that span multiple U.S. regions and international partners.

Why GGBS?

The GGBS Framework emerged from decades of research, teaching, and applied projects where “green,” “grey,” “blue,” and “smart” infrastructure were often considered separately—yet were required to work together in practice.

GGBS was developed to:

  • Provide a coherent, integrative structure for water and urban systems;
  • Connect site-scale actions to watershed, estuarine, and coastal dynamics;
  • Combine engineering design, environmental performance, and societal needs;
  • Offer practitioners and students a clear language for integrated planning.

The framework is deliberately practical: its value lies in helping real communities, agencies, and students think systematically about cross-scale and cross-sector solutions.

GGBS does not replace existing standards or models—rather, it brings them into a unified structure that highlights interactions, synergies, and opportunities for co-benefits.

Threads Connecting Research, Teaching & Practice

The GGBS Framework reflects several threads that consistently appear throughout Professor Guo’s career:

  • Green stormwater and urban hydrology: experiments, monitoring, modeling, and GI design guidance;
  • Watershed and basin-scale strategies: integrated approaches for rivers, floodplains, and sediment;
  • Estuaries and coasts: tides, salinity intrusion, storm surge, waves, and sediment transport;
  • Flood risk & resilience: interactions across pluvial, fluvial, and coastal hazards;
  • Education and mentoring: multi-decade contributions to stormwater, hydrology, and coastal courses;

Collaboration & Knowledge Sharing

GGBS.ai is intended as a living resource and collaborative space. Over time, it will:

  • Organize publications, reports, infographics, and videos;
  • Share case studies and applications to cities, watersheds, and coasts;
  • Provide tools, datasets, and example workflows;
  • Highlight student and partner contributions where appropriate.

The goal is to make practical, scientifically grounded knowledge easier to access and apply.

Engaging with GGBS

Opportunities to work with or apply the GGBS Framework include:

  • Integrating GGBS into municipal, regional, or coastal resilience plans;
  • Adapting conceptual diagrams for teaching, training, or policy presentations;
  • Collaborating on case studies, scenario analyses, or comparative evaluations;
  • Exploring applications involving digital twins, monitoring networks, or AI-based tools.
